Establishment and development of mantle geochemistry

地幔地球化学的建立与发展

2026-01-29
Science China Earth Sciences (IF: 5.0)

Lihui Chen，Xiaoyu Zhang，Jian Zhao

DOI: 10.1007/s11430-025-1820-6

Mantle geochemistry, a field that emerged in the mid-1970s, utilizes geochemical approaches to investigate the chemical composition and structure as well as the evolution and dynamics of Earth’s mantle. Since its establishment, this field has become a key part of solid Earth Science and Planetary Science, demonstrating great vitality. The development of its theoretical framework began with the observation of mantle heterogeneity revealed by the radiogenic isotopic compositions of ocean island basalts (OIBs) and mid-ocean ridge basalts (MORBs), which in turn gave rise to the classic “crustal recycling” model. Based on this, a suite of globally representative mantle end-member components, namely DMM (Depleted MORB Mantle), EMI (Enriched Mantle I), EMII (Enriched Mantle II), HIMU (High μ, μ=238U/204Pb) and PREMA (prevalent mantle), was identified, thereby establishing a comprehensive framework to characterize the geochemical structure and geodynamic system of the mantle. Over the past five decades, mantle geochemistry has advanced with new analytical techniques and theoretical development, greatly broadening its scope. It is now a key field within the Earth System Sciences, crucial for developing models of deep material cycling and surface environmental responses. Aiming to review the development of mantle geochemistry, this paper analyzes eight seminal studies focused on radiogenic isotopes, revisiting the establishment and evolution of mantle geochemistry, exploring the scientific vision of pioneers and highlighting the key topics that define this discipline.

Ba isotope unmasks recycling processes of fluid mobile elements in subduction zones

钡同位素揭示俯冲带流体活动元素的再循环过程

2026-01-27
Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta (IF: 4.5)

Yang Yu，Xiao-Long Huang，Betchaida D. Payot，Hai-Quan Liu，Ying-De Jiang 等

DOI: 10.1016/j.gca.2026.01.038

Geochemical recycling processes in subduction zones exhibit significant variability due to the incorporation of complex subduction components into the mantle wedge. Here, we present whole-rock geochemical and Ba–Sr–Nd–Hf–Pb isotopic data for basalts from the sediment-rich Luzon Arc and the sediment-poor Bicol Arc to constrain the recycling processes of fluid mobile elements (FME) in different subduction settings. Our results suggest that the mantle wedge beneath the Luzon and Bicol arcs was primarily metasomatized by melts from subducted sediments and fluids from subducted oceanic crust, respectively. The basalts from both arcs show variable δ 138/134 Ba values despite their limited variations in Sr–Nd–Hf–Pb isotopes, reflecting the heterogeneous dehydration of phengite in subducted sediments and oceanic crust, which have undergone different extents of Ba isotopic fractionation at shallow depths. Luzon back-arc basalts (BAB) exhibit Sr–Nd–Hf–Pb isotopic compositions similar to those of island arc basalts (IAB), yet possess more variable and lighter Ba isotopic compositions. This defines a distinct Ba isotopic lightening trend across the arc, reflecting progressive Ba isotopic fractionation associated with enhanced phengite dehydration at increasing subduction depths. The Bicol basalts show lower 87 Sr/ 86 Sr and much higher Sr/Zr ratios compared to the Luzon basalts, indicating pervasive fluid infiltration from the subducted oceanic crust into their mantle sources, which promotes the preferential breakdown of lawsonite and results in significant Sr enrichment in the mantle wedge. This observation explains why global arc basalts tend to have higher Sr/Zr ratios with decreasing 87 Sr/ 86 Sr ratios. Notably, Luzon basalts display Ba/Zr, K/Zr, and Rb/Zr ratios that largely overlap with those of Bicol arc basalts. We propose that subducted oceanic crust plays a role similar to that of subducted sediments in recycling FME, such as K, Rb, and Ba, owing to the buffering effect of phengite. Nevertheless, it potentially contributes more significantly to Sr enrichment within the mantle wedge of subduction zones.

Sulfate-driven anaerobic oxidation of methane in deeply buried sediments on the Southern Campbell Plateau

南坎贝尔高原深埋沉积物中硫酸盐驱动的甲烷厌氧氧化

2026-01-27
Chemical Geology (IF: 3.6)

Alex J. Reis，Vanessa Fichtner，Andrea M. Erhardt

DOI: 10.1016/j.chemgeo.2026.123269

Sulfur is a key component in the reduction of organic carbon in marine sediments. Sulfate-driven anaerobic oxidation of methane and microbial sulfate reduction are the leading processes driving the removal of organic carbon and sulfur from the environment. Microbial sulfate reduction has been well studied in most marine depositional environments, however, studies examining sulfate-driven anaerobic oxidation of methane are primarily limited to shallow burial or methane seep environments. Sediments recovered from IODP site U1553 on the southern Campbell Plateau demonstrate evidence of a deeply buried sulfate-methane transition zone (SMTZ). In this study, we identified a deep (> 500mbsf) sulfate-methane transition zone through the analysis of sulfur isotopes in sulfide minerals (d 34 S CRS ). Three potential paleo-SMTZs were also identified as peaks in d 34 S CRS below the modern SMTZ, suggesting the changing seafloor and depositional conditions since the late Oligocene contributed to the upward migration of the SMTZ over time. Understanding the evolution of deep SMTZs and the processes associated with them will help further constrain how the sulfur and carbon cycles interact and drive carbon storage in marine sediments.
